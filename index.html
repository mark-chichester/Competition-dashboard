              Understanding expected scores

              This model is designed to estimate what a dance might score at a competition based on patterns in past results. It looks at factors such as similar routines, competition scoring trends, judging panels, and how a dancer or routine has performed over time. In simple terms, it answers the question: “Given everything we know, what score would we expect here?”

              It’s very important to understand what this is—and what it isn’t. This model is not a measure of how “good” a dancer or dance is. Dance scoring is inherently subjective. Judges bring their own opinions, preferences, and interpretations into every score. Two judges can watch the same routine and score it differently, and both can be valid. This model simply works with those subjective scores and tries to find patterns within them.

              The expected score should be thought of as a contextual benchmark, not a judgment. It represents what similar routines have typically received under similar circumstances. It is built from a combination of factors, including the average scores for similar dancers (baseline), the way a specific competition tends to score, judging panel tendencies, style and subscore trends, and performance history. It also considers age relative to peers, so dancers are evaluated within the context of their group—not against everyone equally.

              For example, if a routine has an expected score of 287.9 and receives a 289.4, that doesn’t mean the dancer is suddenly “better” or that the judges were right or wrong. It simply means that, compared to similar routines and past data, this performance scored slightly higher than what would typically be expected. A small difference like this is completely normal and happens all the time because judging is subjective.

              In general, scores that are within about a point of the expected score are very typical. Differences of one to two points are still quite normal. Larger differences can happen, but they usually reflect judging variation, performance differences on the day, or specific competition context—not a definitive change in ability.

              The goal of this model is not to label or define dancers. Instead, it exists to provide perspective and context. Competition scores can feel very meaningful, but they are just one interpretation from a specific panel on a specific day. This tool helps put those numbers into a broader context, so dancers and teachers can focus less on individual scores and more on long-term growth, consistency, and performance quality.

              🔍 Example: How an Expected Score is built

              To make this more concrete, here’s a simple walkthrough of how the model builds an expected score for a solo.

              Imagine a routine called “Gravity”.

              We start with a baseline. This comes from similar solos—same level, age group, and category. Based on past results, those routines tend to score around 287.0. That gives us our starting point.

              Next, we adjust for the competition itself. Some competitions score a little higher or lower overall. If this event tends to run slightly high, we might add +0.8, bringing us to 287.8.

              Then we consider the judging panel and style trends. If this panel tends to score slightly generously, that might add +0.4. If routines like this (based on style or subscore patterns) have been trending a bit higher, that could add another +0.3. Now we’re at 288.5.

              After that, we look at performance history. If this dancer has been consistently scoring above average recently, the model reflects that with a small adjustment—say +1.0. That brings us to 289.5.

              We also account for age relative to peers. This isn’t a large adjustment, but it helps place the dancer within their group rather than against everyone. In this case, it might shift the score slightly (for example, −0.3), bringing us to 289.2.

              Finally, the model applies small balancing steps to keep scores realistic—preventing values from drifting too high or too low compared to real competition results.

              🎯 Final Expected Score

              After combining all of those factors:

              👉 Expected Score ≈ 289.2

              📊 How to read it

              If the routine actually scores:

              • 289.2 → exactly as expected
              • 290.5 → slightly above expectation
              • 287.5 → slightly below expectation

              Again, this doesn’t mean right or wrong—it simply shows how that score compares to typical outcomes in similar situations.

              💡 Key takeaway

              Each expected score is not coming from a single rule or opinion. It’s built from many small pieces of context layered together:

              • what similar dancers score
              • how that competition behaves
              • how judges tend to score
              • how the dancer has been performing

              Put together, these create a best estimate, not a definitive answer—and always within a system that is ultimately subjective.
